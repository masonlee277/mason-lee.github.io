About Me

I'm Mason Lee, a Machine Learning and Applied Math student at Brown University with a passion for applying AI to environmental challenges. My background spans from wildland firefighting to cutting-edge research in machine learning for earth systems. I'm particularly interested in using deep learning techniques to tackle climate change and improve disaster response.

My work experience includes roles at NASA, where I've applied machine learning to fluid dynamics and wildfire research, and at Brown University, where I've contributed to projects in river connectivity and disaster migration dynamics. I'm also the co-founder of Pluto Los Angeles, an experimental event space that has given me unique experience in project management and marketing.

Outside of academics and work, I'm an avid big wall climber, Jiu Jitsu practitioner, and DJ. I'm always looking for new challenges and opportunities to apply my skills in machine learning, environmental modeling, and software engineering to real-world problems.

Improving Probabilistic Model Based Reinforcement Learning Control in Chaotic Environments with Conditional Normalizing Flows

Description

This research introduces a novel model-based reinforcement learning algorithm called CNF-MC-PILCO. It uses Gaussian Processes and Conditional Normalizing Flows to model system dynamics and estimate policy gradients using a Monte Carlo approach.

Description

This study presents the application of multi-fidelity learning and Bayesian optimization for predicting sea surface temperature. It implements two models: a multi-fidelity neural network and a multi-fidelity Gaussian Process, comparing their performance in data-sparse environments.

Description

This paper proposes a novel approach to model-based reinforcement learning by combining probabilistic models with physics-based models. Sensitivity of simulated wildfire spread to parameter uncertainty and spatial resolution of terrain and fuel maps

Description

This study examines the sensitivity of wildfire spread simulations to various parameters and spatial resolutions. It uses the ELMFIRE model to simulate the 2020 Creek Fire in California, exploring the impacts of spotting, wind conditions, and fuel characteristics on fire behavior predictions.
