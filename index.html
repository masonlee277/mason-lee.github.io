About Me

Hey I'm Mason. I love to solve challenging earth science problems using machine learning.

I believe that our relationship with the planet will be changed by machine learning and allow us to navigate its shifting, chaotic nature. In order for humanity to thrive and live in tandem with nature, we need a full understanding of the Earth's dynamical processes. By building a full picture of how our actions affect the planet and vice-versa, I have hope we can cultivate a lasting symbiotic relationship.

Here is a bit of background on myself: I served as an initial attack wildland firefighter during the most intense wildfire season in California history, spending months working to protect my community from deadly fires. At the end of that year, I co-founded Pluto, an event venue in Los Angeles. At Brown University and NASA, much of my research focused on wildfire applications and then expanded to broader geospatial machine learning problems that can be solved using large remote sensing datasets. Earth Science fascinates me because of its complexity: it's a partially observable, chaotic, and poorly understood system that is tightly coupled with multi-scale physics at every level. We are now just finally creating the toolset at scale to properly explore these planetary scale problems.

To understand, solve, and control these complex process, I have a strong conviction we need convergence of our first principles (physics), our data (remote sensing), and our modeling tools (ML/DL) to be able to make probabilistic predictions and control systems that are robust and scalable.

Other cool things: I'm an avid big wall climber, Jiu Jitsu practitioner, and DJ.

  • Snow-Cover Area and Runoff Variation in Northwest Greenland: This study analyzes the correlation between snow cover frequency, snow albedo, and runoff patterns in Northwest Greenland using MODIS satellite data and field measurements, finding that mean snow albedo over the ice sheet is strongly negatively correlated with runoff and a more important factor than snow cover alone.
  • GlacialVision: Ultra-High Resolution SupraGlacial RiverMaps using a Dual-UNET Architecture. Solved a long-standing problem of river connectivity deliniation from satellite retrievals to improve water routing in climate models. Operationalized a dual-UNET architecture for ultra-high resolution supraglacial river mapping in an inference endpoint.
  • PhyDream: Enhancing Model-Based Reinforcement Learning with Jacobian Alignment in Differentiable Physics Engines. We propose a novel approach to enhance world models in Model-based Reinforcement Learning (MBRL) by incorporating physics priors through a differentiable physics engine, Brax. Our method introduces a Jacobian Alignment loss function that aligns gradients from Brax and neural networks, potentially improving the fidelity of world models and leading to more effective reinforcement learning systems..
  • FlowControl: Model-Based Policy Optimization for Active Flow Control. The implementation of Model-Based Reinforcement Learning (MBRL) for active flow control, integrating a dynamics model ensemble with physics-informed losses, a Soft Actor-Critic policy optimizer, and a TCP-based interface to a wind tunnel environment, aiming to optimize actuator control for reducing flow separation.

Selected Research

  • Improving Probabilistic Model Based Reinforcement Learning Control in Chaotic Environments with Conditional Normalizing Flows

    In this paper, we present a Model-Based Reinforcement Learning (MBRL) algorithm named Conditional Normalizing Flows Monte Carlo Probabalistic Inference for Learning COntrol (CNF-MC-PILCO). The algorithm relies on Gaussian Processes (GPs) and Conditional Normalizing FLows (CNF) to model the system dynamics and on a Monte Carlo approach to estimate the policy gradient.

  • Fourier Adaptive Learning for Control In Turbulent Fluid Dynamics

    This work delves into the domain of adaptive control through the lens of Fourier analysis, concentrating on the challenges posed by turbulent environments where reinforcement learning is traditionally employed. Conventional neural network models, despite their powerful capabilities, frequently encounter difficulties such as overfitting, limited observability, and hindered generalizability. To navigate these challenges, we implement the recently successful Fourier Adaptive Learning for Control (FALCON) algorithm, an approach that synthesizes Fourier methods with control theory. Our implementation is unique in its integration of L1 and L2 regularization strategies within the reinforcement learning framework to foster sparsity and precision in the learned models. Additionally, we incorporate a customized CrossEntropy Method to suit the FALCON structure, enhancing the model's ability to handle the intricacies of dynamic systems. The paper presents a comprehensive suite of evaluation tools, offering detailed insights into the model's performance, stability, and adaptability.

  • Graph Neural Networks for Prediction of Human Migration Response to Natural Disaster Events.

    Using GNNs to understand the affect of natural disaster in intra-county migration in the US. We employ three variations of Graph Neural Networks (GNNs): spatiotemporal message passing neural network (LSTM-MPNN), non-temporal MPNN, and temporal diffusion convolution, which facilitates the learning of patterns of migration behavior and the formulation of predictions for the current or upcoming year.

  • Parrallel Simulated Annealing Optimization in Heterogenous Environments

    This paper explores parallel implementations of Multi-Start Simulated Annealing (MSA) and Coupled Simulated Annealing (CSA) for optimizing the non-convex Schwefel function in heterogeneous computing environments. The researchers compare naive single-threaded implementations to optimized versions using function inlining, OpenMP thread parallelization, and CUDA GPU kernels. Experiments on 10 and 100 dimensional optimization landscapes demonstrate significant speedups from parallelization, with GPU implementations reducing minimization time to about one-third of the CPU-only version. The results highlight the potential of heterogeneous computing for accelerating complex optimization problems, particularly for high-dimensional, linearly separable functions.

  • Multi-Fidelity Bayesian Neural Networks for Sea Surface Temperature Prediction

    This study presents the application and exploration of multi-fidelity learning and Bayesian optimization in the context of environmental data prediction and analysis. We successfully applied multifidelity learning to approximate the Forester function, demonstrating the advantages of leveraging both low- and high-fidelity data. Furthermore, we implemented active learning within a Bayesian optimization framework, showcasing the potential to reduce uncertainty with minimal sampling. This work includes the implementation of two models, a multi-fidelity neural network (MF-NN) and a multi-fidelity Gaussian Process (MF-GP), for sea surface temperature prediction.

  • SINDy-PETS: A Flexible Physics-Informed and Probabilistic MBRL Architecture

    Model-based reinforcement learning (MBRL) has shown promise in addressing the limitations of traditional model-free reinforcement learning, particularly in terms of sample efficiency and the ability to backpropagate through learned differentiable environments. However, MBRL often falls short in performance due to model bias, which arises from the inability to accurately represent the environment, exploding particle rollouts, and inaccurate state-representations. In this paper, we propose a novel approach to MBRL by combining probabilistic models with physics-based models to capture environmental uncertainty while benefiting from the sample efficiency brought about by a physical bias. We introduce two methods for incorporating physics-informed bias into Probabilistic Ensembles with Trajectory Sampling (PETS): Residual Correction (RC) and Opinion Input (OI). Our approach uses the Sparse Identification of Nonlinear Dynamics (SINDy) algorithm to learn the physics model with a robust backend to inject useful properties such as conservation of mass, energy and momuentum. SINDy-PETS is most useful in data-sparse environments, especially rigid body systems governmed by ODEs / PDEs.

  • Sensitivity of simulated wildfire spread to parameter uncertainty and spatial resolution of terrain and fuel maps

    This study explores wildfire spread modeling using the ELMFIRE simulation tool, focusing on the 2020 Creek Fire in California as a case study. The research employs Latin Hypercube Sampling and Sobol Sequence Sampling to efficiently explore the parameter space of fire behavior variables, conducting thousands of simulations across different resolutions. Remote sensing data from sources like VIIRS and NIROPS is used to validate fire perimeter predictions, with model performance assessed using metrics like Intersection Over Union (IOU) coefficients. Statistical techniques including ANOVA, Generalized Additive Models, and Sobol's sensitivity analysis are applied to understand parameter importance and interactions, with a particular focus on spotting behavior and its impact on fire spread.

  • Methanotrophic bioremediation for the degradation of oceanic methane and chlorinated hydrocarbons

    Methane (CH4) has been underrepresented as a contributor to the global warming effect related to carbon dioxide (CO2). Carbon dioxide is about 200 times as abundant in the atmosphere, however volume alone does not give a clear picture of its environmental impact. Methane is much more immediate in the way it damages the earth as a greenhouse gas, with most of its absorbed heat generated in the first 20 years after emission where carbon dioxide has an environmentally damaging "slow burn" over what typically lasts several hundred years. When methane is removed from the atmosphere, we will see the effect much more immediately, and have a chance to help generations in the much less distant future. The objective of this study was to develop a novel, low-cost biotrickling filter able to degrade methane as well as co-metabolize carcinogenic chlorinated hydrocarbons. While methanotrophic bioremediation has been adapted for in situ degradation in freshwater, there has never been an ex situ remediation system tailored for the degradation of methane in marine environments. The dynamic and modular biotrickling filter demonstrates the feasibility of methanotrophic bioremediation for the degradation of oceanic methane and chlorinated hydrocarbons in our waterways and oceans.
